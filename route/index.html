How Quantum Routing Differs

Classical routing algorithms explore solution spaces sequentially. Quantum approaches like QAOA leverage superposition to evaluate multiple routes simultaneously, and interference to amplify optimal solutions.

Grover's search provides a quadratic speedup (√N vs N) for unstructured search, while quantum annealing exploits quantum tunneling to escape local minima that trap classical methods.

The VQE Router uses a variational ansatz to encode tour permutations, optimized via SPSA gradient estimation. Quantum Walk exploits wave-like interference across multiple walkers to explore the solution landscape in parallel.

For N cities, the TSP search space is N!/2N. Even with N=20, this is ~6×10¹⁶ routes. Quantum parallelism can explore this exponential space in polynomial depth circuits — a fundamental advantage for logistics at scale.

Understanding route optimization algorithms

🗺️ The Problem

Route optimization (a variant of the Traveling Salesman Problem) asks: "Given a set of delivery stops, what is the shortest route that visits each stop exactly once and returns to the depot?" This is NP-hard — the number of possible routes grows factorially with stops.

Dijkstra + NN

O(V² log V) O(V) Not Optimal

Computes the shortest-path tree from the depot using Dijkstra's algorithm, then constructs a tour by always visiting the nearest unvisited node. The single-source shortest paths ensure accurate distance computation even through road networks.

🔬 Classical vs Quantum

Classical Approach

  • Explores solutions one-at-a-time
  • Heuristics trade accuracy for speed
  • Exact solvers limited to ~20 nodes
  • Gets trapped in local optima
  • Well-understood, mature tooling

Quantum Approach

  • Superposition evaluates many routes simultaneously
  • Interference amplifies better solutions
  • Quantum tunneling escapes local minima
  • Grover provides quadratic speedup
  • Hardware-limited today, massive future potential

🏭 Real-World Applications

Route optimization impacts every logistics company on earth. Amazon, FedEx, and UPS collectively solve millions of vehicle routing problems daily. A 1% improvement in route efficiency for a major carrier can save $50M+ annually. As quantum hardware matures, quantum-optimized routing will become a critical competitive advantage in supply chain management.

🧠 When To Use Each Algorithm

< 15 stops: Use Held-Karp or Branch & Bound for exact optimal solutions.
15–50 stops: Use 2-Opt or Simulated Annealing for near-optimal results in practical time.
50–200 stops: Use Genetic Algorithm with large population sizes. Consider QAOA or VQE for potential speedup.
200+ stops: Use Greedy NN + 2-Opt as baseline. Quantum Annealing and Quantum Walk show the most promise at this scale.
